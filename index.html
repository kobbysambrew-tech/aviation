Ghana National Air

A Ghanaian-owned international airline built around the Airbus A350-1000. Long-haul routes from Accra and Kumasi to key global cities — with profits, ownership and control staying in Ghana.

Our Mission

The mission of Ghana National Air is to give Ghana its own world-class long-haul airline, not just rely on foreign carriers. We want to turn Accra—and eventually Kumasi—into real international hubs that connect West Africa to Europe, North America and the Middle East. Instead of sending ticket money abroad, Ghanaian investors will own the aircraft and share in the returns.

The International Opportunity

West Africa has growing demand for international travel, but most long-haul seats are controlled by non-African airlines. Ghanaians flying to London, New York, Dubai or Amsterdam usually travel on foreign planes. A Ghanaian-owned A350-1000 operation allows us to capture part of that demand and keep value, jobs and tax revenue in Ghana.

Why Airbus A350-1000

Flagship Widebody

The A350-1000 is Airbus’ latest large long-haul aircraft. It typically carries around 350–410 passengers with a modern, quiet cabin. Using this aircraft immediately positions Ghana National Air as a serious, premium international airline.

Range & Performance

With a range near 9,000 nautical miles, the A350-1000 can operate routes like Accra–London, Accra–New York (with possible initial one-stop), Accra–Dubai and other major global links. This range gives flexibility for future route growth.

Efficiency & Image

Advanced materials, aerodynamics and engines lower fuel burn compared to older widebodies. At the same time, the A350-1000 has a modern “flagship” image that helps attract higher-yield passengers and partnerships.

Kumasi: Growing Second Hub

Kumasi’s Prempeh I International Airport has been undergoing major expansion, including a new terminal designed to handle around one million passengers a year and runway extension to around 2,320 meters. That turns Kumasi from a purely domestic airport into a serious regional and limited international gateway, supporting future routes and feeding traffic into long-haul flights from Accra.

Cabin Experience

The long-haul cabin concept includes full flat-bed business class, a comfortable premium economy section, and a modern economy cabin with personal screens at every seat. The color scheme, uniforms and onboard service can highlight Ghana’s culture so passengers feel at home the moment they board.

Phase 1 Routes — Long-Haul

Initial focus is on a small number of high-demand international routes: Accra–London, Accra–Dubai, and Accra–a North American gateway (such as New York or Toronto, possibly starting with a one-stop option). The goal is to keep the operation tight and focused while building reliability and brand recognition.

Accra + Kumasi Network

Over time, domestic and regional flights from Kumasi, Tamale, Takoradi and other cities can feed long-haul departures from Accra. Kumasi’s upgraded airport makes it easier for passengers in the middle belt and northern regions to access international flights without always having to connect through foreign hubs.

Operational Timeline (High-Level)

  1. 0–6 months: Finalize shareholder structure, refine business plan, begin Airbus discussions, engage regulators and key partners.
  2. 6–12 months: Place orders or secure lease agreements for two A350-1000s, start recruitment and training, design brand and cabin product.
  3. 12–24 months: Take delivery of aircraft, complete route certifications and test flights, and launch limited long-haul services from Accra.

Ghanaian Shareholder Model

The vision is for Ghanaians—individuals, pension funds, institutions and diaspora investors— to own the airline. Shareholders would supply the capital that would normally come from foreign banks, and in return they receive equity, dividends when the airline becomes profitable, and a voice in major decisions.

Governance & Transparency

Strong governance is essential, especially at this scale. An independent board, audited accounts, and transparent reporting are built into the model. Major spending and fleet decisions are approved at board and shareholder level, reducing opportunities for corruption and misuse of funds.

Aircraft Cost — Big Picture

The official “list price” of an Airbus A350-1000 is a bit over USD 360 million per aircraft. Airlines normally negotiate large discounts, or use leasing structures, but even with discounts this is a very expensive plane. For planning purposes, we can assume a net acquisition cost (after negotiation or lease structure) in the range of roughly USD 250–280 million per aircraft.

Illustrative Startup Budget

These are simplified, rounded numbers for discussion — not final quotes. They show roughly how much capital would be needed to launch and survive the first phase:

  • • Approx. USD 520 million for two A350-1000s (combined net purchase / lease value).
  • • Around USD 60 million for training, systems, spare parts, marketing and setup.
  • • Around USD 90 million as an 18-month operating reserve (fuel, crew, maintenance, fees).

Revenue Model (Illustrative)

Revenue comes from premium and economy tickets on long-haul routes, plus cargo and partnerships. Early years focus on achieving good load factors and building trust. As the brand stabilizes, yields and ancillary revenue can improve, lifting margins.

How Profits Could Be Shared

A simple approach is to split profits between dividends, reinvestment and reserves. The exact percentages can be adjusted with investors, but the key idea is that Ghanaian shareholders see clear, fair benefit from the success of the airline.

Market Opportunity

International demand to and from Ghana and the wider region is driven by business travel, tourism, education and the diaspora. Capturing even a modest share of West Africa–Europe, West Africa–Middle East and West Africa–North America flows can support a focused two-aircraft long-haul operation.

Brand & Marketing Approach

The brand can lean heavily into Ghana’s colors, culture and hospitality. Marketing would focus on the Ghanaian and West African diaspora, corporate partners, travel agencies and tourism boards. A simple, reliable product with honest communication is more important than expensive gimmicks.

Risk & Reality Check

This is a high-risk project. Widebody long-haul operations are extremely capital-intensive and sensitive to fuel prices, exchange rates, political changes and competition. The numbers shown here are rough planning figures, not guarantees. Any real launch would require deep due diligence and professional airline management.

Investor Materials

A detailed prospectus, financial model and risk analysis can be prepared for serious investors. Those documents would go into more detail on financing structures, phasing, and possible alternatives if a full A350-1000 launch is considered too aggressive.
